Maxwell's Equations Basics

What is a Vector Field

Scalars describe the magnitude of a phenomena, while vectors describe both magnitude and direction. Vector fields are functions that assign a vector to every point \((x,y,z)\) in space. A bold face symbol will indicates a vector or vector field. Electromagnetic problems are separated into two temporal categories, arbitrary time and harmonic time. Real life usually adheres to arbitrary time dependence. For example if a switch in a circuit is closed at time \(t\), the time dependence of the voltage would be a step function. Arbitrary time dependant quantities will be represented with script symbols \(\t{A}\). Combining time and vector notations, an arbitrary time dependant vector field is written like \begin{equation*} \tv{A}=\tv{A}(x,y,z;t) \end{equation*} It is important to keep in mind that vectors are abstractly defined as the combination of magnitude and direction. However, they are commonly described in terms of a coordinate system. In Cartesian coordinates, a general vector \(\v{v}\) is projected onto the Cartesian unit vectors \(\v{v}=v_x\uv{x}+v_y\uv{y}+v_z\uv{z}\). Where \(v_x,v_y,v_z\) are the vector components. Vector fields can also be written in component form \begin{equation*} \tv{A}=\t{A}_x(x,y,z;t)\uv{x}+\t{A}_y(x,y,z;t)\uv{y}+\t{A}_z(x,y,z;t)\uv{z} \end{equation*} Where \(A_x,A_y\) and \(A_z\) are component functions of space and time instead of numbers like in the single vector case. A vector with a hat indicates it is a unit vector \(\uv{v}\). For example the unit vectors of the cartesian coordinate system are written as $$ \uv{x}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix}\quad \uv{y}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}\quad \uv{z}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}\quad $$

Quantities and Units

  • The electric field intensity \(\tv{E}\), \(\frac{\text{V}}{\text{m}}\)
  • The magnetic field intensity \(\tv{H}\), \(\frac{\text{A}}{\text{m}}\)
  • The electric flux intensity \(\tv{D}\), \(\frac{\text{C}}{\text{m}^2}\)
  • The magnetic flux intensity \(\tv{B}\), \(\frac{\text{Wb}}{\text{m}^2}\)
  • The source (impressed) electric current density \(\tv{J}_i\), \(\frac{\text{A}}{\text{m}^2}\)
  • The electric conduction current density \(\tv{J}_c\), \(\frac{\text{A}}{\text{m}^2}\)
  • The source (impressed) magnetic current density \(\tv{M}_i\), \(\frac{\text{V}}{\text{m}^2}\)
  • The electric charge density \(\t{q}_{\text{ev}}\), \(\frac{\text{C}}{\text{m}^3}\)
  • The magnetic charge density \(\t{q}_{\text{mv}}\), \(\frac{\text{Wb}}{\text{m}^3}\)

Time dependent Maxwell's Equations

Maxwells equations takes the quantities from above and relates them via a set of first order, coupled partial differential equations. \begin{align} \nabla \times \tv{E} &= -\tv{M}_i - \frac{\partial \tv{B}}{\partial t} \\ \nabla \times \tv{H} &= \tv{J}_i + \tv{J}_c + \frac{\partial \tv{D}}{\partial t} \\ \nabla \cdot \tv{D} &= \t{q}_{\text{ev}} \\ \nabla \cdot \tv{B} &= \t{q}_{\text{mv}} \end{align}

Time harmonic Maxwell's Equations

The general time dependent Maxwell's equations are often needlessly complex. General periodic time dependence can be constructed with the Fourier transform. So instead electromagnetic quantities are expressed in a purely spacial dependent form. Where \begin{equation} \tv{E}=\text{Re}\left[\v{E}(x,y,z)e^{j\omega t}\right] \end{equation} This simplification leads to the time harmonic Maxwell's equations \begin{align} \nabla \times \v{E} &= -\v{M}_i - j \omega \v{B} \\ \nabla \times \v{H} &= \v{J}_i + \v{J}_c + j \omega \v{D} \\ \nabla \cdot \v{D} &= q_{\text{ev}} \\ \nabla \cdot \v{B} &= q_{\text{mv}} \end{align}
